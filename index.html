Introduction

The quality of the air around us has a significant effect on our daily life and health. Today, exposure to air pollution is the fifth greatest risk factor of mortality globally, and every year 4.2 million people die prematurely from outdoor air pollution. Data reveal that 99% of the world’s population live in areas exceeding WHO’s guidelines for clean air.

Whether you are affected by air pollution depends on where you live. On a global scale, those living in countries with fewer economic resources are also suffering the most from air pollution. However, when zooming in on Denmark to examine more granular patterns, the global tendency does not seem to translate. This project is thereby indicating that environmental inequality is a complex matter, where detailed investigation is needed to obtain an understanding of who the affected individuals are and protect the public health in the future.

The project is divided into two parts: First, we examine pollution and inequality globally. Then, we zoom in on Denmark to investigate patterns in a country with high GDP and relatively low concentrations of air pollution. We will mainly focus on pollution caused by PM(2.5), also known as fine inhalable particles. PM(2.5) is the pollutant causing the greatest risk for human health by causing cardiovascular and respiratory disease. PM(2.5) is emitted from power plants, industries, and automobiles, and are thereby direct consequences of human behavior.
